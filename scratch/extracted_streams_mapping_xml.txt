Section 1 — Strategic Context
Ginashe Academy is a pan-African skills academy operating from ginashe.academy, incorporated as Ginashe Academy (Pty) Ltd. The Academy began as the Digital Systems Stream (DSS), focused on cloud computing through the Cloud Launchpad programme. With the acquisition of the ginashe.academy domain and the strategic decision to expand beyond the digital niche, DSS becomes one of ten streams within a broader institution serving the most urgent skill demands of South Africa and the African continent.
This document — Phase 1 of 4 — presents the stream mapping. It sets out which streams will be established, why each is strategically important for the South African and continental context, and the order of priority for launch.
Subsequent documents in the Strategic Blueprint cover:
Phase 2 — Curriculum Blueprint: detailed course structures, NQF levels, and delivery models for all 80+ courses.
Phase 3 — Delivery, LMS & Revenue: delivery channels, accreditation roadmap, LMS architecture, facilitator training, and the revenue model.
Phase 4 — Governance, Brand & Web: board structure, brand identity system, website architecture, partnership framework, and legal structure.
Selection criteria
The 10 streams were selected and ranked using four criteria:
Skills gap urgency — how acute is the SA shortage in this skill area?
Employment yield — does graduating from this stream produce immediate employment or entrepreneurial pathways?
Continental relevance — does the skill transfer beyond SA into the broader African market?
Delivery feasibility — can Ginashe Academy realistically deliver this stream at scale within 24 months?
Tier classification
Each stream is classified into one of three tiers:
Flagship (5 streams) — immediate SA need, launching in Year 1.
Strategic (4 streams) — 2 to 5 year growth horizon, launching in Year 1 to 2.
Emerging (1 stream) — continental scale opportunity, launching in Year 2.
Section 2 — The Ten Streams
The streams are presented in priority order. Stream #1 — Digital Systems (DSS) — is already operational. Streams #2 to #5 are flagship streams to be launched in Year 1. Streams #6 to #9 are strategic streams for Year 1 to 2 launch. Stream #10 is the emerging stream for Year 2.

Section 1 — How to Read This Document
This is the curriculum blueprint for Ginashe Academy (Pty) Ltd. It is the working detail behind Phase 1's stream mapping — what learners will actually study, for how long, in what mode, and toward which job outcomes.
Document structure
The document is organised by stream. Each stream occupies its own section and follows the same structure:
Stream banner — name, abbreviation, mission, and rank.
Summary block — learning pathways, cross-stream synergies, and the stream's overall course profile.
Course cards — one per course, showing name, NQF level, duration, delivery mode, modules, graduate outcome, and entry requirement.
Course delivery modes
Three delivery modes are used across the curriculum:
In-person — physical campus or workshop attendance, used for hands-on practical courses (trades, hardware, clinical practice).
Online — fully digital delivery via the Ginashe Academy LMS, used for theory-heavy and individually-paced courses.
Hybrid — mixed delivery, usually online theory plus in-person practical sessions or workshops.
Course modes — cohort versus self-paced
Courses operate in one of two scheduling modes:
Cohort — fixed start and end dates, group of learners moves through the course together with the same facilitator. Higher completion rates, stronger peer support.
Self-paced — learner enrols any time and progresses at their own pace. Useful for working professionals, supported by asynchronous facilitator office hours.
Workshop — short, intensive courses (4–10 weeks) usually delivered as a single block. Used for skill-specific upskilling.
NQF levels used in this curriculum
All courses are aligned to the South African NQF (National Qualifications Framework). Levels in this curriculum range from L2 to L6:
NQF Level 2–3 — foundational. Suitable for learners with Grade 9–10. Equivalent to lower secondary school competence.
NQF Level 4 — basic occupational. Equivalent to matric or N3. Most stream courses sit here.
NQF Level 5 — intermediate occupational. Equivalent to first-year tertiary or N4–N5. Many specialist courses sit here.
NQF Level 6 — advanced occupational. Equivalent to advanced certificate or diploma. Reserved for high-level technical and leadership courses.

Section 2 — Delivery Model
Ginashe Academy delivers learning through four complementary channels. Different learner contexts require different channels — a rural smallholder farmer cannot attend a Johannesburg campus weekly, and a working professional in Sandton does not need a mobile learning unit. The four channels work together to reach every learner segment in South Africa and the broader continent.
The four delivery channels
Community campuses
Physical hubs in townships, peri-urban, and rural areas
Shared equipment, fibre connectivity, facilitator-led cohort model. Prioritised for hands-on streams — Applied Trades, Health Sciences, Agriculture. Each campus operates as a learning community, not a building.
Online-first LMS
Mobile-first platform reaching every SA province
Self-paced and cohort courses delivered via the Ginashe Academy LMS. Designed for low-bandwidth South African networks. Facilitator office hours twice weekly. Used heavily by Digital Systems, FinTech, and Creative Media.
Employer partnerships
B-BBEE skills spend cohorts
Corporates fund cohorts for their staff or community pipeline. Customised curriculum, on-site or hybrid delivery, bursary and stipend models. Aligns with B-BBEE skills development scorecard.
Mobile learning units
Solar-powered campus-on-wheels
Converted vans with solar power, StarLink internet, and laptop sets. Rotates through rural municipalities on a weekly schedule. Brings the campus to the learner. Critical for Agriculture, Energy, and Digital Literacy.
Learner support infrastructure
Delivery channels alone are not enough. South African learners face structural barriers — cost of data, lack of devices, transport costs, family responsibilities, and economic precarity. Ginashe Academy addresses these directly:
Stipend model
Removing the financial penalty of learning
Unemployed learners receive a stipend of R1,500 to R3,000 per month during contact learning. Funded through SETA discretionary grants, the National Skills Fund, and employer B-BBEE levy contributions. Without stipends, the most vulnerable learners cannot afford to attend even free courses — they need to eat and travel during the learning period.
Device programme
Refurbished laptops on loan
Learners who do not own a personal computer receive a refurbished laptop on loan for the duration of their course. 75% of the device cost is subsidised by corporate partners as part of B-BBEE enterprise development spend. At course completion, the learner can purchase the device for R500 or return it for the next cohort.
Data bundles
Removing the bandwidth barrier
Online learners receive a 5 GB monthly data bundle for the duration of their course. Ginashe Academy negotiates zero-rated access to ginashe.academy with MTN, Vodacom, and Telkom — meaning learners can use the LMS without consuming their personal data allowance. WhatsApp integration for assignment submission ensures even data-poor learners can participate.

Section 4 — LMS Architecture
The Ginashe Academy LMS is hosted at learn.ginashe.academy. It is the primary channel for the online-first delivery model and the secondary channel for hybrid courses. The architecture is designed for South African network conditions — mobile-first, offline-capable, low-bandwidth, with WhatsApp integration for the most data-poor learners.

Academy management system

Low-bandwidth design principles
The LMS is built for South African network conditions — patchy coverage, expensive data, intermittent connectivity. Three design principles dominate every technical decision:
Offline-first
PWA with service worker
The LMS is built as a Progressive Web App with a service worker that caches lessons locally. Learners download content when they have connectivity, then study offline. The LMS syncs assignments and progress automatically when the learner reconnects. Designed to function on 2G connections at minimum.
Video compression
Adaptive bitrate schooling
All video content uses adaptive bitrate schooling. Mobile users default to 360p; the player only requests higher quality on stable Wi-Fi. Every video has a transcript for text-based study. Audio-only mode lets learners study during commutes when video data costs are prohibitive.
WhatsApp integration
Reaching the data-poor learner
A custom WhatsApp bot lets learners submit assignments, take quizzes, ask the AI tutor questions, and receive facilitator notifications via WhatsApp. For learners whose data plans only cover WhatsApp (the majority of low-income SA users), this is the difference between participation and exclusion.
Section 5 — Facilitator Pipeline
The quality of an academy is the quality of its facilitators. Ginashe Academy operates a four-level facilitator competency framework — sourcing, training, and progressing facilitators systematically.
Four-level competency framework
Entry
Level 1 — Subject Matter Expert
Industry professional with 3+ years of practical experience. Assessed through portfolio review. Recognised Prior Learning pathway. Paid per session — typically R600 to R1,200 per session for senior practitioners.
After 4 weeks
Level 2 — Registered Facilitator
Completes the Ginashe Facilitator Foundations programme (4 weeks). Achieves ETDP SETA unit standard 117871 (Facilitate Learning). Assigned to cohorts as primary facilitator. Available part-time or full-time.
After 12 months
Level 3 — Master Facilitator
Mentors Level 1 and Level 2 facilitators. Designs new course modules. Contributes to SETA evidence packs. Higher session rate plus benefits package. Eligible for stream-level decision-making roles.
After 2+ years
Level 4 — Stream Lead
Owns curriculum quality for an entire stream. Manages academic partnerships. Carries P&L responsibility for stream revenue. Reports to the Academic Director. Typically a small number of these roles per stream (1–2).
Facilitator Foundations programme — 4 weeks
Every Ginashe Academy facilitator completes the Foundations programme. It is not optional. Industry expertise alone does not equal teaching capability — adults learn differently, classrooms have dynamics, assessments have rigour, and SETA paperwork has deadlines.
Week 1 — Adult Learning Theory
How adults learn differently from children
Andragogy versus pedagogy. Active learning principles. The South African adult literacy context. How to assess incoming learners' actual level (which often differs from their formal qualifications). Why "learning by doing" beats "learning by listening" for working-age adults.
Week 2 — Lesson Design
From course brief to deliverable lesson
Backwards design — start with the assessment, work backwards to the lesson plan. Lesson plan templates aligned to Ginashe LMS structure. SA-specific examples in every lesson. Assessment design — formative versus summative. LMS content creation hands-on workshop.
Week 3 — Facilitation Skills
Running a real classroom or video session
Managing diverse cohorts (different ages, backgrounds, prior knowledge). Asking questions versus telling answers. Microphone presence and screen discipline for video. Handling conflict, disruption, and emotional moments in the classroom. Learner support and wellbeing.
Week 4 — Assessment & SETA
The paperwork that protects the qualification
Formative versus summative assessment in detail. Portfolio of Evidence (PoE) requirements per SETA. Marking guides and moderation. SETA forms and reporting deadlines. Academic integrity. Learner appeals process. The non-negotiable compliance basics.
Where facilitators come from — sourcing strategy
Alumni-to-facilitator — top graduates from each cohort are invited into the facilitator track 12 months after graduation, once they have employment experience. This creates a self-reinforcing pipeline of practitioners who already know the Ginashe method.
Industry practitioners — part-time facilitation by working professionals. Cloud engineers, nurses, electricians, farmers. Authentic industry experience in every classroom. The biggest advantage Ginashe has over traditional academic institutions.
University partnership — final-year and postgraduate students from Wits, UCT, UJ, and Tshwane do supervised facilitation as part of their community engagement credits. Lower cost to the Academy and meaningful experience for the students.

Section 1 — Governance Structure
Ginashe Academy currently operates as Ginashe Academy (Pty) Ltd — a private company. The recommended structure is for Ginashe Academy (Pty) Ltd to operate as a wholly-owned subsidiary of Ginashe Group (Pty) Ltd, the holding company. This structure provides legal clarity, operational efficiency, and future flexibility — including the option to convert to or to spin out a sister NPC entity at the right time.
Board of Directors
Even as a Pty Ltd, Ginashe Academy operates with a properly constituted board. The board is the legal, fiduciary, and strategic governing body of the company. Five director roles, with a sixth subcommittee for finance:
Chair
Chairperson — Independent non-executive
Provides governance, fiduciary oversight, and public credibility. External to Ginashe Group operations. Appointed for an initial three-year term, renewable once. Recommended for the first board even though not legally required for a small Pty Ltd.
MD
Managing Director — Ginashe Group nominee
Holds the operational and strategic mandate. Day-to-day accountability for delivery of the Academy's mission. Maintains the relationship between Ginashe Group (parent) and Ginashe Academy (subsidiary).
NED-1
Non-executive Director — Industry nominee
Brings industry credibility and employer relations. Curriculum quality input from a market perspective. Typically a senior practitioner from one of the Academy's priority sectors (cloud, energy, health, agriculture).
NED-2
Non-executive Director — Academic nominee
Brings academic rigour and accreditation expertise. Provides external moderation guidance. Typically a senior academic from a SA university or an experienced SETA accreditation specialist.
COMM
Community Representative
Elected by the learner alumni body each year. Provides the learner voice at board level. Ensures the Academy's mission stays grounded in learner reality, not boardroom abstraction. May join formally once the alumni body reaches sufficient size.
FIN
Finance Committee
Subcommittee of the board. Oversees SETA reporting, grant expenditure, audit findings, and partner reporting. Meets quarterly. Reviews accounts before they go to the full board. Critical even at Pty Ltd stage — SETA expects this oversight to exist.
Academic Council
Where the board governs the institution, the Academic Council governs the curriculum. Curriculum decisions cannot be made by financial signatories alone — they require academic and industry input.
Academic Council
Quarterly governance of curriculum and assessment
Chaired by the Academic Director. Comprises all 10 stream leads plus 2 external academics. Approves curriculum changes, new programmes, assessment standards, and external moderation reports. Meets quarterly. Empowered to escalate concerns directly to the board.
External Moderation Panel
Per SETA accreditation requirement
Reviews 10% of all marked assessments per cohort. Conducts annual curriculum quality review per stream. Members are SETA-approved and entirely independent of the Academy. Critical for maintaining provisional and full SETA accreditation.
Student Representative Council
Monthly governance from the learner perspective
Elected by each cohort. Provides the formal feedback channel from learner to leadership. Meets monthly with the Academic Director. Participates in programme reviews. Empowered to raise concerns to the Community Representative on the board.
Ginashe Group relationship
The recommended structure is for Ginashe Academy (Pty) Ltd to be a wholly-owned subsidiary of Ginashe Group (Pty) Ltd. This is the cleanest configuration for the early years and offers four advantages over running the Academy as a separate, independent entity:
Brand and credibility transfer — the Group's existing relationships, vendor contracts, banking infrastructure, and credibility transfer to the subsidiary instantly. A separate independent entity must rebuild all of this from scratch.
Tax efficiency on early-year losses — early-stage Academy losses can be group-relief'd against Ginashe Group profits in the same financial year. As a separate independent entity, those losses sit unused on the books for years before becoming valuable.
Operational simplicity — shared services (IT infrastructure, finance, HR, legal counsel) flow naturally between parent and subsidiary without elaborate management-fee paperwork or transfer-pricing complexity. Auditors and SARS understand and accept this structure routinely.
Future optionality — if the Academy later requires NPC conversion (when SETA grants and donor money become significant) or external investment, separating a subsidiary is far simpler than restructuring an independent entity. The subsidiary can also operate alongside a future sister NPC for the bursary or grant arm.
Service-level agreement between parent and subsidiary
Required even within the same group
A formal SLA between Ginashe Group and Ginashe Academy documents the management services provided (IT, finance, HR), the fees charged (transparent, market-rate), and the delivery commitments. This is required by SARS for transfer-pricing compliance and by SETAs as evidence of operational independence at the curriculum level.
Core policy framework
Every training provider in the South African education sector must maintain a defined policy framework. Ginashe Academy's framework consists of nine documents that are reviewed annually by the Academic Council and approved by the board:
Academic integrity policy — defines plagiarism, AI-assisted work boundaries, and assessment fraud.
Learner rights and responsibilities charter — issued to every learner at intake.
Facilitator code of conduct — defines professional standards for every facilitator.
POPIA privacy policy — Information Officer appointed, data subject rights documented.
Whistleblower protection policy — anonymous reporting channel for ethics concerns.
Conflict of interest register — declared annually by every board member and employee.
Grievance and appeals procedure — formal route for learner complaints and assessment appeals.
Safeguarding policy for minor learners — applies to ECD school and any course with under-18s.
Inclusion and accessibility policy — disability accommodation, language support, gender equity.

Logo construction notes
The shield-form symbol uses cyan (#00F2FF) as its fill, with a 7px outline that matches the active text colour (Night for light variant, White for dark variant).
A bold "G" sits inside the shield, drawn at a 14px stroke-width, in Night colour for both variants.
Circuit-pattern detail flanks both sides of the shield as decorative elements that animate in web/digital contexts (CSS pulse animation) and remain static in print.
A small antenna-like glyph above the shield (the diamond + curved stroke + small square) signals connection, signal, broadcast — a subtle nod to digital identity without dominating the mark.
The wordmark "GINASHE" uses Montserrat 900 with negative tracking to create density. "ACADEMY" uses Outfit 700 with widely tracked letters (0.5em) to create breathing space.
A horizontal cyan signal line separates the two words. In digital contexts, this line carries an animated white pulse that travels left to right every 10 seconds.
Logo variations and minimum sizes
Primary lockup (horizontal): minimum width 120px digital / 30mm print. Used for headers, signage, presentations.
Stacked lockup (symbol above wordmark): used where horizontal space is constrained — apps, square ads, social profiles.
Symbol-only mark (shield with G): used as favicon, app icon, social avatar. Minimum 16px.
Wordmark-only: used in monochrome contexts where the symbol cannot reproduce cleanly — embossed or single-colour print.
Reversed/monochrome variants: White on any solid colour for legibility on photography overlays.
Logo do-nots
Do not stretch, skew, or recolour the symbol — cyan is the only acceptable fill.
Do not place the logo on background colours that compromise the symbol's contrast — minimum WCAG AA contrast required.
Do not separate the symbol from the wordmark in primary contexts — the lockup is the lockup.
Do not animate the wordmark — only the cyan signal line is animated.
Do not use the stream accent colours as the symbol fill — cyan is reserved for the master mark only.

Typography
Two typeface families serve the entire Ginashe Academy system — Montserrat and Outfit. Both are open-source Google Fonts, ensuring consistent rendering across web, mobile, print, and document templates without licensing barriers.
Montserrat — display and headlines
Montserrat 900 (Black weight) for the wordmark, primary headlines, and high-impact display text. Negative tracking creates the density that makes "GINASHE" feel substantial. Montserrat 700 (Bold) for secondary headlines and section dividers.
Outfit — body and UI
Outfit 400 (Regular) for body copy across LMS, documents, and web. Outfit 500 (Medium) for emphasis. Outfit 700 (Bold) for sub-headings and the "ACADEMY" lockup. Outfit's geometric character pairs cleanly with Montserrat without competing for visual weight.
Document fallback — Arial
All Word document templates use Arial as the primary typeface for compatibility. Where Montserrat or Outfit is unavailable (printed by a third party, viewed on a system without Google Fonts), Arial Bold and Arial Regular provide a clean visual fallback that matches the geometric character of the brand fonts.
Photography direction
Photography is the most powerful brand asset because it carries the human reality of the Academy. Stock photography of American office workers actively damages the brand — it makes Ginashe Academy look like every other generic education business. The photography brief is strict.
Real Ginashe Academy learners in real Ginashe Academy environments. Workshops, clinics, farms, township campuses, mobile learning units. Never rented sets, never staged.
Natural light preferred over studio lighting. Photography that looks like it could be a documentary still — not a marketing brochure.
Candid moments over posed portraits. A learner concentrating on a soldering joint. A facilitator pointing at a circuit diagram. A graduate hugging a parent at a ceremony.
Every South African demographic represented across the body of work. No tokenism — the photography body should look like SA looks.
Action over passivity. Action shots beat headshots. Hands working, eyes thinking, mouths explaining.
Joy is permitted. Transformation looks like confidence and pride — not struggle. Avoid the saviour-narrative photography that other education brands lean on.
Dignity-first framing. Never frame a learner as someone to be rescued. Always frame them as someone building their own future.

Six standard partnership clauses
Every partnership agreement — regardless of tier — includes these six clauses. They are non-negotiable. Their purpose is to protect what cannot be replaced if compromised: curriculum independence, learner data, and the Academy's reputation.
Clause 1 — Curriculum independence
No partner may dictate or approve curriculum content
Ginashe Academy retains full editorial and curriculum control. No sponsor may require curriculum changes, mandatory product placement, brand endorsements, or modified assessment criteria. Violation of this clause terminates the agreement immediately, with no penalty payable by the Academy.
Clause 2 — Learner data sovereignty
Learner data belongs to the learner, not the partner
Learner personal data is owned by the learner. Partners receive only aggregated, anonymised outcomes data. Individual learner data is never shared with funders, employers, or third parties without explicit, informed, written learner consent. Full POPIA Condition 2 and Condition 7 compliance is mandatory.
Clause 3 — Employment commitment
Funded cohorts come with an interview pipeline obligation
Employer partners who fund cohorts commit to interviewing all qualifying graduates within 60 days of cohort completion. This is an interview commitment, not an employment guarantee. The commitment is publicly reported in the annual report — partners are listed by name and interview rate.
Clause 4 — B-BBEE reporting
SETA-compliant skills spend certificates within 30 days
Ginashe Academy provides SETA-compliant skills spend certificates within 30 days of cohort completion. Certificates specify BEE ownership status, SETA alignment, NQF level, and learner demographics — sufficient detail for direct B-BBEE scorecard inclusion by the partner.
Clause 5 — Quality assurance rights
Observation, not interruption
Partners may observe (not interrupt) any class or assessment session with five business days written notice. Partners receive quarterly outcome reports. Curriculum or assessment concerns are raised through the Academic Council — never directly to facilitators or learners.
Clause 6 — Branding and attribution
Partner recognition without brand confusion
Partners are listed as sponsors on relevant course pages and graduate certificates. Partners may not brand themselves as co-deliverers of the qualification — only as funders, employers, or supporters. The Ginashe Academy mark is the primary identity on all learner-facing materials.
Section 5 — Legal & Corporate Structure
Ginashe Academy currently operates as Ginashe Academy (Pty) Ltd, a private company under the Companies Act 71 of 2008. This section sets out the current structure, the recommended near-term configuration as a wholly-owned subsidiary of Ginashe Group, and the future-state options that may make sense as the Academy scales — including potential conversion to or sister-NPC creation.
Current structure
Ginashe Academy (Pty) Ltd
Private company under the Companies Act
Currently incorporated as a Pty Ltd. This is the right structure for the early years — operationally simple, commercially flexible, and well-understood by SARS, banks, and partners. Maintains the founder's direct decision-making authority and avoids the governance complexity of an NPC at a stage where the institution is still finding its operating rhythm.
Recommended near-term configuration
The recommended structure for the next 18 to 36 months is for Ginashe Academy (Pty) Ltd to operate as a wholly-owned subsidiary of Ginashe Group (Pty) Ltd. This is a minor structural change from independent Pty Ltd to subsidiary Pty Ltd, but it unlocks meaningful advantages.
Parent
Ginashe Group (Pty) Ltd — holding company
The existing holding company. Owns the Ginashe brand and intellectual property. Provides operational management services to the subsidiary on a transparent management fee basis. Maintains the broader Ginashe Group corporate identity and existing commercial relationships.
Sub
Ginashe Academy (Pty) Ltd — operating subsidiary
Wholly-owned subsidiary of Ginashe Group. Operates all 10 streams. Generates revenue from learner fees, corporate B-BBEE cohorts, government contracts, and (where eligible as a Pty Ltd) selected SETA programmes. Maintains its own bank accounts, audit, and statutory returns.
SLA
Group Service Agreement
A formal service-level agreement between Ginashe Group and Ginashe Academy documents the management services provided (IT, finance, HR, legal), the fees charged at market rate, and the delivery commitments. Required by SARS for transfer-pricing compliance and by SETAs as evidence of operational independence at curriculum level.
Why subsidiary is better than independent for now
Brand and credibility transfer — the Group's existing relationships, vendor contracts, banking infrastructure, and credibility transfer to the subsidiary instantly. A separate independent entity must rebuild all of this from scratch.
Tax efficiency on early-year losses — early-stage Academy losses can be group-relief'd against Ginashe Group profits in the same financial year. As a separate independent entity, those losses sit unused on the books for years.
Operational simplicity — shared services flow naturally between parent and subsidiary without elaborate paperwork. Auditors and SARS understand and accept this structure routinely.
Future optionality — converting to NPC or creating a sister NPC is significantly easier from a subsidiary structure than from an independent Pty Ltd. The subsidiary can also accept outside investment in the future without restructuring the entire group.
Future-state options — when and why to consider change
The current Pty Ltd structure is right for now. Three future scenarios may make sense to consider at the appropriate moment, none of them urgent:
Future option A — Convert Ginashe Academy to NPC
Trigger: when SETA grants and donor money exceed 30% of revenue
A full conversion of Ginashe Academy from Pty Ltd to NPC unlocks SETA discretionary grant eligibility, Section 18A donor receipts, PBO tax exemption, and B-BBEE CSI/ED contribution recognition. Worth considering when grant and donor revenue would exceed 30% of total revenue and the loss of commercial flexibility is justified by the funding gain.
Future option B — Create a sister NPC alongside the Pty Ltd
Trigger: when bursary endowment or donor funding reaches material scale
A separate Ginashe Academy Foundation NPC operates alongside the Pty Ltd. The NPC handles bursaries, donor funding, and SETA grants. The Pty Ltd handles commercial training, corporate cohorts, and content licensing. Best of both worlds — preserves commercial flexibility while unlocking grant funding. More complex to manage but increasingly common for SA training providers operating at scale.
Future option C — Create a Trust for the bursary endowment
Trigger: when capital donor commitments justify a separate vehicle
A Ginashe Academy Trust holds the bursary endowment fund. Receives large capital donor contributions. Distributes annual bursaries per the deed of trust. Tax-efficient vehicle for philanthropic giving — donors receive Section 18A certificates if the Trust is registered as a PBO. Typically established when capital donor commitments reach R5 to R10 million plus.
Annual compliance — current Pty Ltd obligations
As a Pty Ltd, Ginashe Academy maintains an annual compliance calendar with five recurring requirements. These are the same obligations that apply to every active South African Pty Ltd:
CIPC annual return — submitted within 30 business days of the company's registration anniversary date.
SARS annual income tax return (ITR14) — submitted within 12 months of financial year end.
Annual financial statements — prepared and signed off by directors. Audit required only if the public interest score exceeds the threshold; otherwise an independent review is sufficient.
POPIA Information Officer registered with the Information Regulator — already required regardless of company type.
B-BBEE certificate or sworn affidavit — annual renewal. Affidavit acceptable for EME and QSE thresholds.
When SETA accreditation is achieved, additional SETA-specific compliance obligations apply per the Phase 3 accreditation roadmap.
